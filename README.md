I officially hit 900 hours of documented lab time this week. When I started this 1,000 hour project a year ago, the goal was simple. I wanted to build a technical foundation so deep that my competence would never be a question of credentials, but a matter of demonstrated skill.

The last 100 hours have been about internalizing the most difficult sections of the CPTS path like Active Directory, pivoting, and thick client exploitation. I went back through a domain compromise assessment that originally took me 12 hours in January and finished it in 4 hours and 43 minutes. The difference wasn't about remembering the path. It was about having the technical intuition to know exactly which tools to use for the specific context.

To keep this organized, I built a CPTS Methodology Field Manual. It has become my second brain for prep boxes and covers everything from initial reconnaissance to complex pivoting. I even included a section called "The Wall" for the specific steps I take when I am stuck on a box.

I built this manual because in a real engagement, value isn't measured by what you can memorize in a vacuum. It is measured by how you navigate the unknown. Cybersecurity moves too fast for ego. Success comes from having a repeatable methodology that works when the standard answers don't.

This methodology is what allowed me to push into higher level attack chains recently. I have been working on ESC16 certificate impersonation, using Shadow Credentials through GenericWrite permissions, and chaining AS-REP roasting with RC4 downgrades.
